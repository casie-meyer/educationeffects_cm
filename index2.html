The Effects of Education Choropleth Maps

The next step of investigating these correlations is creating choropleth maps and comparing them. This gives the opportunity to view the data spatially, look for spatial trends, and see what places experience the same dataset trends. A choropleth map was made for each each variable being investigated, each with the quantile classification scheme. For the maps to be comparable with the equal-interval and Jenks Natural Breaks schemes, the datasets need to have the same ranges and be distributed similarly along a number line. This is not the case with each variable's dataset. The choropleth maps classified with a quantile scheme allowed for comparison of maps and uncovering of some spatial trends. State's Population of Bachelor's Degree Holders

This map was created to use as a comparison for other variables in this study. A choropleth map was created, using a quantile classification scheme, to show the percentage of populations holding bachelor's degrees or higher in each state. The dark blue states on the map represent high educated populations, and the light blue states represent low educated populations. There are a few spatial patterns to glean from this initial map. States with high educated populations are clustered in New England, possibly because of the increased number of prestigious universities in those states. One other notable region where many states with high educated populations are clustered is the US's west coast. This may be because of the increased number of technical companies in that region that employ people who need a college degree. Besides the coasts, there are states with high (Colorado, Kansas, Minnesota) and low (Wyoming, Nevada, Indiana) educated populations scattered around the middle of the US.

State's Population Living Under the Poverty Line

This choropleth map of the state's population living below the poverty line (annual household income of $24,999 or less), was created with a quantile classification scheme to compare to the educated population choropleth map. On the map, the dark blue states represent high populations living under the poverty line, and the light blue states represent low populations living under the poverty line. When compared to the education map, a clear trend presented itself. In general, states with higher educated populations have lower populations living under the poverty line (Colorado, New Hampshire, Maryland, Delaware, Washington, Minnesota, Utah), and vice versa ( Oklahoma, Arkansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, Kentucky, West Virginia). When looking at the maps side-by-side, the colors of the maps are mostly inverted. This makes sense, because increased educational attainment is generally correlated with higher incomes. If more of a state's population is educated, more of the population should have higher annual incomes, which would lead to lower populations living under the poverty line.
